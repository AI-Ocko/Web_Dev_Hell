---
title: "The Woman in the Wheelchair"
number: 1
date: 2026-04-26
---

As I've made my trips to and from East Pasadena to work and home, I've grown acquainted with the many homeless, "unhoused," or whatever you'd prefer to call them, around the area.
They span a great range of characters, but one of them has lingered in my mind to this day, as I somehow got wrapped up in helping them find their way home.

Her name escapes me.
She was an older woman, by my guess, in her forties or fifties.
I had just gotten off work when I found her stuck on the side of the curb by the cross walk, seemingly unable to move her wheelchair, which was acting as her own luggage stacking God knows how much beer, trash, and whatever else she was taking with her.
She was struggling with the wheelchair, I think it had gotten stuck on the curb.
I asked if she needed help, and she just nodded while continuing to struggle.
I lifted the wheelchair, and set her straight.
The crosswalk light had turned, and I offered to walk her through.
What a gentleman.
I would soon realize there was a big problem.
This lady probably needed the wheelchair more than the garbage that was sitting in the seat.
She could barely walk, seeing as how we made slow work of the intersection.
She limped sluggishly, and I would later learn she had some accident earlier in the day.
I guess she somehow ended up alongside a freeway bridge and had fallen.
I'm not sure of the specifics, but I was sure as shit confident that this lady seriously needed to get off the leg she was using.

Eventually, we finally made it to the other side of the street, not after the light had turned and we had barely made it halfway.
I asked her where she was going, and she told me "home."
She was dropped off by a kindly bus driver, who took her to the nearest Ralph's, as that's where she told the driver she lived near.
I knew there was a Ralph's on the on adjacent side of the street, and that, begrudgingly, I'd have to help her across that intersection as well.
I'd already made it this far, what's another intersection?
I confirmed with her that that was where she was trying to go, and off we went.

As we made it to the other side, the local leecher who I see stand on the median on my way to work came up to us, tearfully thanking me for what I'd done for the lady.
He said he'd rarely seen any kindness like mine, and that people like me were rare nowadays.
It was a simple act, but such simplicity would soon turn complicated before I even knew it.

We continued up the street to where she thought she lived.
Yes, thought.
You see, not only was this woman partially disabled, but also partially confused.
She wasn't familiar with the area at all, which greatly concerned me.
I had just taken an extra ten minutes out of my day to help her across two crosswalks, and apparently, this is not where she was supposed to be.
I asked her what her address was, and when I put the address she gave me into my phone, I soon realized what I had gotten myself into.

The woman didn't live in East Pasadena, she lived right in the heart of the city.
Her home was miles away from where we were; we were nowhere near where she needed to be.
My mind soon began thinking about my next actions.
Was I going to leave this lady and say, "Welp, I tried helping, but I gotta get home now"?
Was I going to take her back to the nearest bus station and hope another kindly bus driver would help her in my stead?
Or would I seriously take this lady all the way back home, walking the entire distance to wherever this address on my phone was?

Seeing this rest of the words on this page, you can probably guess what I did. 

I began thinking about skipping the gym, and even my homework to make the time to help this poor old woman.
I looked at the distance Maps said it would take.
I thought about what my other options were, and if there were any way I could reasonably get out of this without feeling bad about it.

Fortunately for me, I did find one trick that would help me out.
The Metro line ran practically right next to where she lived.
And seeing as how I would be walking the distance to the station anyways, taking this lady with me would basically be no problem at all.
Sure, it would take a little longer, but it was already on the way.
I'd just have to make two quick stops, walk a little ways to her address, and then be on my way.
I had a plan!

I helped the lady back down the street, and across the intersection one last time.
After a bit more walking together down the road to the station, I eventually decided it would be faster if I just moved the trash in the chair, and put her in it. Try to move things a long more efficiently.

This would backfire on me later.

We would make our way down the road, crossing the street a few more times.
It was at a certain crossing did I realize what potential misfortune I would have from putting this poor lady in the seat.
You see, moving the trash only gave a few inches of sitting room in the wheelchair.
She was practically on the edge the entire time.
I guess she had grown tired, maybe from all the walking we'd done earlier, or maybe from the accident she had earlier in the day.
Anyhow, her core strength was extremely lacking, to say the least.
I would be made painfully aware of this as we went down the decline in the sidewalk, bumping over the cracks in the road, as she would tip over and fall onto her face into the concrete road.

Fuckkkkkkk.

I apologized profusely as I picked her up, and helped her back into the chair.
Thankfully, she didn't seem too hurt.
I felt awful, and I told her I'd be more careful.
I should mention she seemed somewhat out of it for the entirety of my encounter with her.
I don't know if she had been drinking earlier, but if she had (given the excess empty beer cans she'd been carrying in her chair), I hope that helped with the pain.
We continued on, inevitably reaching the train station.

I was making a bit of conversation with her when we turned the corner.
It was at this time that I learned her name that I can now no longer remember, as well as an interesting fact about her.
She was a troublemaker, she told me.
She would get in fights with some of the girls that went to her school.
She seemed visibly distressed about it, almost like she had a longing regret.
At least that's what I made of it.

Down the decrepit bus stops on the bottom floor, onto the elevator smelling like piss, and through the gate which everyone but me would skip paying for.
We had made it to the platform, and after a bit of waiting, we boarded the train.
The ride was short, but I remember feeling a wave of relief.
We were practically halfway.
While putting her in the wheelchair certainly led to some undesirable outcomes, it certainly made getting here a lot faster.
I can't imagine what it would've been like had I had to suffer her hobbling all the way to the station.
Truth be told, she probably wouldn't have made it.
Whatever the case, we were nearly there.

As we exited the car, we took another elevator, and we were back on the street level.
I checked the map again, and her place wasn't that far away.
Two blocks down, a left-turn, a few more blocks, and it was there.
We set off.

I took in the sights of the buildings around us. I don't live a rural life by any means, but I definitely don't live near enough tall buildings to get used to them.
Buildings high enough to block out parts of the sky always fill me with a small sense of awe.
Los Angeles always gives me that greater feeling, and I was happy finding it again here in the middle of Pasadena.
I think of it now, as I write this, and I'm reminded of her.

We made our way down the streets, and turning left, we passed a Ralph's.
This must've been the Ralph's the woman was talking about.
Perhaps the bus driver who helped her earlier simply thought of the nearest Ralph's, and had hoped that it was the right guess.
That was horribly mistaken buddy, you were pretty far off.

Past a few more streets, and we were close.
I was checking the map once more, before I realized we were coming down another steep transition from pavement to asphalt.
I had felt those familiar bumps from the road in the wheelchair again.
But it was too late, the lady had leaned too far forward, and had fallen face flat once again.

God dammit.

I helped her back up, and apologized, profusely.
What a dumbass I was.
Thankfully, we were basically at the address she had given me.

It was at this moment I had started to question the validity of the address itself, because there was no home here that I could recognize.
Gee, wouldn't it be fantastic if we had come all this way just for this place to be the wrong place?!
Thankfully, it was the correct address.

The lady reassured me, and as I read the words on the building that was located at the address, I reassured myself.
It was a shelter, one hosted by a branch of the Salvation Army.
There were sliding doors on the side of the building, and there we waited, because she had forgotten her keys.

I'm not sure how we got in.
I think someone who worked there happened to pass by, and asked us what we were doing.
The person was a younger woman herself, and she immediately recognized the lady I was with.
She started asking her questions about how she had gotten out, and what she had been doing all day.
From what I can remember, they were calling her phone, and the lady had given the keys to her apartment to some man that the other lady had previous knowledge of, given that she was scolding the poor woman for "giving her keys to that man again."
The specifics are blurry to me now, but we had entered the building, into another elevator, and eventually reached the main floor with all the housing units.
We walked up to her place, and they got the door open.

Seeing the state of the room was sad.
There were beer cans strewn about the floor.
Old, crusty food was left in several pans on top of the stained stove.
The bed at the end of the room was sheet-less.
The floor itself was dirty, the flat-screen TV still had the purchase sticker across its face.

I wasn't sure what to think.
I felt bad.
It didn't occur to me at the time the reality of how some people live.
I knew shelters existed, but I guess I hadn't really thought about what they're actually like, or who the people that live in them are.
This lady was miserable, drowning her apparent sorrow in cheap liquor.
All I could do was take it all in.

The other lady, the one who let us in, asked the woman if she needed to be taken to the hospital.
She refused; it's no wonder.
After a bit more settling, I said my goodbyes, and headed home.

---
This happened two years ago, during my first year working at Jersey Mike's.
It was my first, real, intimate encounter with a homeless person.
I guess she wasn't really "homeless," but close enough.
I had always known people like her existed, but I never really grasped the reality until that day.
I used to have a sort of disdain for them.
People like her.
People that were washed up drunkards who spent their time bathing in the sun.
Perhaps bathing is all they can do, given the state of their poor physical extremities.

The point is, I hadn't given it a real thought.
Until I met her, I was ignorant.
Now, I'm not sure what to think.
I only feel guilt.
